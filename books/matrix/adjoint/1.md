The **adjoint** of a square matrix (also very commonly called the **adjugate matrix** or **classical adjoint**) is the **transpose of its cofactor matrix**.

This is the standard meaning in most undergraduate linear algebra courses when people say "matrix adjoint" or "adjoint of a matrix".

**Notation**: Usually written as `adj(A)` or sometimes `Adj(A)`.

### Formal definition

Let A be an n × n square matrix.

1. For each entry a_{ij}, compute its **cofactor** $C_{ij}$ 
   $C_{ij} = (-1)^{i+j} × det(M_{ij})$
   where $M_{ij}$ is the $(n-1)×(n-1)$ submatrix obtained by deleting row i and column j.

2. Form the **cofactor matrix** $C = [C_{ij}]$

3. The **adjoint / adjugate** is the transpose of that cofactor matrix:  
   $adj(A) = C^T$
   (i.e. the $(i,j)$-entry of $adj(A) is C_{ji}$)

### Most important property

If A is invertible, then

**$A^{-1} = (1 / det(A)) × adj(A)$**

This is the main reason students learn how to compute the adjoint — it's the classical way to find the inverse (especially useful by hand for 2×3 or 3×3 matrices).

### Quick examples

**2×2 case**  
Let $A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}$

Cofactors:  
$C₁₁ = d,   C₁₂ = -c\\
C₂₁ = -b,  C₂₂ = a$

Cofactor matrix = $\begin{pmatrix} d & -c \\ -b & a \end{pmatrix}$

Adjoint = transpose =$\begin{pmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{pmatrix}$

→ Inverse formula: $A^{-1} = (1/(ad−bc)) × \begin{pmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{pmatrix}$

**3×3 case** (just the pattern — computation gets longer)

For $A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b & c \\ d & e & f \\ g & h & i \end{pmatrix}$

$adj(A) = \begin{pmatrix} 
\det\begin{pmatrix}e&f\\h&i\end{pmatrix} & -\det\begin{pmatrix}d&f\\g&i\end{pmatrix} & \det\begin{pmatrix}d&e\\g&h\end{pmatrix} \\
-\det\begin{pmatrix}b&c\\h&i\end{pmatrix} & \det\begin{pmatrix}a&c\\g&i\end{pmatrix} & -\det\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\g&h\end{pmatrix} \\
\det\begin{pmatrix}b&c\\e&f\end{pmatrix} & -\det\begin{pmatrix}a&c\\d&f\end{pmatrix} & \det\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\d&e\end{pmatrix}
\end{pmatrix}$

(with the correct signs alternating according to the cofactor rule).

### Important note about terminology

In more advanced linear algebra / functional analysis, "adjoint" usually means something different:

- For real matrices with standard dot product → adjoint = transpose ($A^T$)
- For complex matrices → adjoint = conjugate transpose ($A^H$)

This is **not** the same as the adjugate/adjoint discussed above.

So when someone says "adjoint of a matrix" in basic linear algebra context (especially when talking about inverses, Cramer's rule, etc.), they almost always mean the **adjugate** = transpose of cofactor matrix.
