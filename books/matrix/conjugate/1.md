The **conjugate** of a matrix (also called the **complex conjugate** of a matrix) is obtained by taking the complex conjugate of every entry in the matrix.

### Definition

If \( A = [a_{ij}] \) is an \( m \times n \) matrix with possibly complex entries, then its **conjugate** (denoted \( \overline{A} \) or sometimes \( A^* \)) is:

$$
\overline{A} = [\overline{a_{ij}}]
$$

where \( \overline{a_{ij}} \) is the complex conjugate of the scalar \( a_{ij} \).

### Example

Let

$$
A = \begin{pmatrix}
1 + 2i & 3 - 4i \\
-5i & 6
\end{pmatrix}
$$

Then the conjugate is

$$
\overline{A} = \begin{pmatrix}
1 - 2i & 3 + 4i \\
5i & 6
\end{pmatrix}
$$

### Important Related Concepts

1. **Conjugate Transpose** (also called Hermitian adjoint or adjoint matrix):
   $$
   A^H = \overline{A}^T = (A^*)^T
   $$
   - First take the conjugate of each entry, then transpose (or vice versa).
   - This is extremely important in quantum mechanics, linear algebra over complex numbers, inner products, normal matrices, unitary matrices, etc.

2. **Properties** of the conjugate:
   - \( \overline{A + B} = \overline{A} + \overline{B} \)
   - \( \overline{cA} = \overline{c} \, \overline{A} \) for scalar \( c \)
   - \( \overline{AB} = \overline{A} \, \overline{B} \)
   - \( \overline{\overline{A}} = A \) (applying twice gives back the original)
   - \( \det(\overline{A}) = \overline{\det(A)} \)
   - \( \overline{A}^H = A \) (for the conjugate transpose)
