The **cofactor matrix** (also called the matrix of cofactors) of a square matrix **A** is a matrix of the same size where each entry is replaced by its corresponding **cofactor**.

### Key Definitions

- **Minor** $M_{ij}$: The determinant of the $ (n-1)√ó(n-1) $ submatrix obtained by deleting row `i` and column `j` from the original $n√ón$ matrix A.
- **Cofactor** $C_{ij}$: $C_{ij} = (‚àí1)^{(i+j)} √ó M_{ij}  $
  ‚Üí This adds an alternating sign (checkerboard pattern): `positive` when `i+j` is even, `negative` when `i+j` is odd.

The **cofactor matrix** C has entries $C_{ij}$ in position (i,j).



### Step-by-Step Process to Find the Cofactor Matrix

1. For each position `(i,j)` in the original matrix:
   - Delete row i and column j ‚Üí get the minor submatrix
   - Compute its determinant ‚Üí that's $M_{ij}$
   - Multiply by $(‚àí1)^{(i+j)} ‚Üí  C_{ij}$
2. Place $C_{ij}$ in position `(i,j)` of the new matrix.

### Example: 3√ó3 Matrix

Let $A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 & 3 \\ 4 & 0 & 5 \\ -2 & 6 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$

We compute each cofactor:

- $C_{11} = (‚àí1)^{(1+1)}\cdot det\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 5 \\ 6 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = (+1)(0\cdot1 ‚àí 5\cdot6) = ‚àí30$
- $C_{12} = (‚àí1)^{(1+2)}\cdot det\begin{bmatrix} 4 & 5 \\ -2 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = (‚àí1)(4\cdot1 ‚àí 5\cdot(‚àí2)) = (‚àí1)(4+10) = ‚àí14  $
- $C_{13} = (‚àí1)^{(1+3)}\cdot det\begin{bmatrix} 4 & 0 \\ -2 & 6 \end{bmatrix} = (+1)(4\cdot6 ‚àí 0\cdot(‚àí2)) = 24$
- $C_{21} = (‚àí1)^{(2+1)}\cdot det\begin{bmatrix} -1 & 3 \\ 6 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = (‚àí1)(‚àí1\cdot1 ‚àí 3\cdot6) = (‚àí1)(‚àí1‚àí18) = 19$
- $C_{22}= (‚àí1)^{(2+2}\cdot det\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ -2 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = (+1)(2\cdot1 ‚àí 3\cdot(‚àí2)) = 2+6 = 8$
- $C_{23} = (‚àí1)^{(2+3)} \cdot det\begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ -2 & 6 \end{bmatrix} = (‚àí1)(2\cdot6 ‚àí (‚àí1)\cdot(‚àí2)) = (‚àí1)(12‚àí2) = ‚àí10$

- $C_{31} = (‚àí1)^{(3+1)}\cdot det\begin{bmatrix} -1 & 3 \\ 0 & 5 \end{bmatrix} = (+1)(‚àí1\cdot5 ‚àí 3\cdot0) = ‚àí5$
- $C_{32} = (‚àí1)^{(3+2)}\cdot det\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 \end{bmatrix} = (‚àí1)(2\cdot5 ‚àí 3\cdot4) = (‚àí1)(10‚àí12) = 2$
- $C_{33} = (‚àí1)^{(3+3)} \cdot det\begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ 4 & 0 \end{bmatrix} = (+1)(2\cdot0 ‚àí (‚àí1)\cdot4) = 4$

So the **cofactor matrix** is:

$\begin{bmatrix} 
-30 & -14 & 24 \\ 
19 & 8 & -10 \\ 
-5 & 2 & 4 
\end{bmatrix}$



### Quick 2√ó2 Shortcut Pattern

For $A = \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}$, the cofactor matrix is: $\begin{bmatrix} d & -c \\ -b & a \end{bmatrix}$

(Notice it's almost like swapping diagonal elements and negating the off-diagonals ‚Äî same pattern appears in the inverse formula.)

Cofactors are especially useful for:
- Computing determinants via cofactor expansion (Laplace expansion)
- Finding the inverse of a matrix: $A^{-1} = (1/det(A)) √ó adj(A)$
- Cramer's rule
- Advanced topics like matrix derivatives or adjugate properties
