A Primer of Quaternions — Chapter 2

Rotations · Turns · Arc Steps — Hathaway (1896)

Hathaway opens Chapter 2 by carefully distinguishing three related but distinct ideas: rotations, turns, and arc steps. Each is a more refined notion than the previous.

Rotation (Art. 23)
A rotation is specified by an axis through the origin and a dihedral angle. Applied to the unit sphere, the sphere turns as a rigid body about the diameter PP′. The equator is the great circle perpendicular to the axis. The arc of the rotation is a directed arc of the equator from A to A′ — either of the two directed arcs (they differ by a full circle).
Half-arc and the fundamental choice
If the two possible equatorial arcs are bisected at L and M, the two half-arcs ĀL and ĀM are supplementary arcs in opposite directions, each less than a semicircle. The rotation is completely determined by either half-arc. This is the seed of the quaternion: a quaternion encodes a half-arc.
Turn (Art. 28–29)
Let radius OA rotate into radius OB. The turn is the operator that performs this rotation — and its arc is the great-circle arc from A to B on the sphere. The arc uniquely determines the turn (up to the ambiguity of full rotations). Hathaway writes: "the great circle AB, the arc of the turn."
Arc Step
An arc step generalises the flat "step" of Chapter 1 to the sphere. It is a directed arc on a great circle. Addition of arc steps means placing them end-to-end: if arc BC follows arc AB, then AB + BC = AC (just as for flat steps, but on great circles). The sum of two arc steps is in general another arc step on a different great circle.

The central theorem of the chapter: the resultant of two successive turns is itself a turn, and its arc is the "sum" of the two arcs — placed end-to-end on the sphere (Art. 29–30).

Hathaway first proves that two successive rotations compose into a single rotation (Art. 24–25), then translates this into the language of turns and arc steps.

1
Finding the resultant axis. Art. 24: Let radii OB, OC rotate into OB′, OC′. Any axis rotating OB→OB′ must lie on the great circle PKL that bisects great arc BB′ at right angles. Likewise any axis rotating OC→OC′ lies on the great circle PN bisecting CC′. Their unique intersection OP is the axis of the resultant rotation.
2
Determining the resultant angle. The angle of the resultant rotation is found by constructing the marked half-arcs of the two component rotations and placing them sequentially on the sphere (Art. 25–26). The resultant half-arc is their "sum."
3
Turns (Art. 29). The resultant of two successive turns is the turn whose arc is the sum of the two arcs. Formally: if arc AB is the first turn and arc BC is the second, the resultant turn has arc AC — where A, B, C are points on the sphere and the arcs are great-circle arcs placed end-to-end.
4
Arc steps (Art. 30). The half-arc of the resultant rotation equals the sum of the half-arcs of its components. The sum of several arcs is found by replacing any two successive arcs by their resultant arc, continuing until one arc remains — exactly the associative rule for steps from Chapter 1, now on the sphere.
Key theorem — Art. 30
The half-arc of a resultant rotation is the sum of the half-arcs of its components, and the arc of a resultant turn is the sum of the arcs of the components. The sum of several arcs is independent of the order in which pairs are combined (associativity) — but not independent of which arc comes first (non-commutativity, proved in Art. 31).

The deepest geometric result in Chapter 2 is the Spherical Triangle Construction (Art. 27). Hathaway shows how the composition of two successive rotations can be read off from a spherical triangle whose vertices are the axes of the two rotations and the resultant.

The construction (Art. 27)
Let rotation R₁ have half-arc from A₀ to B₀ on great circle AB, and rotation R₂ have half-arc from B₀ to C on great circle BC. Place the half-arcs end-to-end. The spherical triangles A₀B₀C and A₁B′C turn out to be equal (congruent), since corresponding sides about the equal vertical angles at C are equal. This congruence proves that the resultant rotation has a specific axis and angle determined by the triangle.
Vertices of the spherical triangle
The three vertices of the key spherical triangle are:
  • C — axis of rotation R₁
  • A — axis of rotation R₂
  • B — axis of the resultant rotation R = R₂∘R₁
Angles and arcs
If the spherical triangle has vertex angles A, B, C and opposite arc-sides a, b, c, then:
  • R₁ rotates by angle 2C
  • R₂ rotates by angle 2A
  • R = R₂∘R₁ rotates by angle 2π − 2B
This is the quaternion multiplication rule in geometric form.
Why this matters
This theorem — known in modern terms as Euler's rotation theorem via spherical triangles — is exactly what quaternion multiplication encodes algebraically. When Hathaway writes q = q₂q₁, the quaternions encode the half-turns (half-arcs), and their product corresponds to "adding" the arcs by placing the triangle together at B. The Note in Art. 27 says: "This theorem enables one to find the resultant of any number of successive rotations, by replacing any two successive rotations by their resultant, and continuing."

Art. 31 proves geometrically that the composition of two rotations (arc steps) is not commutative in general — doing rotation R₁ then R₂ gives a different result from doing R₂ then R₁ — unless the two arcs lie on the same great circle.

Hathaway's proof (Art. 31)
Let the half-arcs of two rotations be arcs α and β. Form both sums α+β and β+α. The resultant arc C′A′ of α+β and the arc CA of β+α are in general different arcs — same magnitude but opposite direction — which means the two resultant rotations have the same axis but opposite angles. Corollary 2 states: the magnitudes of the resultant arcs in both orders are equal. The only case where α+β = β+α is when the arcs are cocircular (lie on the same great circle).
arc α + arc β ≠ arc β + arc α (in general)
|arc α + arc β| = |arc β + arc α| (magnitudes are always equal)
Geometric intuition
Imagine rotating a book: first rotate 90° around the vertical axis, then 90° around the horizontal axis. Reverse the order: first horizontal, then vertical. The final orientations differ. On the sphere, the two triangles ABC and A′B′C have the same side lengths but the vertex B (resultant axis) moves to a different location.
The commutative exception
Two rotations commute if and only if their rotation axes are parallel (both arcs lie on the same great circle). This is the spherical analogue of the flat fact that two translations commute. Rotations about the same axis always commute (they are just additions of angles).
